Buying an investment property in Israel means financing it under tighter rules than a home you'll live in (see our [LTV limits post](/blog/ltv-limits-israel), investment properties top out at a lower ceiling than a first home), and it means the property needs to actually cash flow, not just appreciate. Three numbers answer three different questions about whether it does.

## Gross yield: what the rent alone tells you

Gross annual yield is simply annual rent divided by the property price. Nothing else. If a property renting for ₪5,600 a month costs ₪1,500,000, that's ₪67,200 a year on a ₪1,500,000 price, a gross yield of about 4.48%.

Gross yield is useful for quickly comparing properties, but it ignores every cost of actually owning the place: no mortgage payment, no maintenance, no insurance, no vacancy. Two properties with identical gross yield can have completely different real economics once those costs are in the picture.

## Net yield: after the recurring costs, before the mortgage

Net annual yield subtracts your recurring ownership costs, typically building insurance, maintenance, and a management fee if you use one, from the rent, before dividing by the price. It still deliberately excludes the mortgage payment itself, because yield is meant to describe the property's own economics independent of how any particular buyer chooses to finance it.

Continuing the example: say building insurance runs ₪1,500 a year and maintenance is budgeted at 5% of monthly rent (about ₪280 a month, ₪3,360 a year), with no management company. Net yield comes to (₪67,200 minus ₪4,860) divided by ₪1,500,000, or about 4.16%. That's the number that actually reflects what the property generates before financing decisions enter the picture.

## Net monthly cash flow and cash on cash return: what financing does to the picture

This is where the mortgage payment enters. Net monthly cash flow is your effective rent, minus the mortgage payment, minus your recurring costs. Using a mortgage payment of roughly ₪4,606 a month (a ₪750,000 loan at 5.5% over 25 years, at the 50% investment LTV ceiling on this property), the cash flow comes to ₪5,600 minus ₪4,606 minus ₪280, or about ₪589 a month, thin but positive.

Cash on cash return takes that annual cash flow (₪589 times 12, about ₪7,068) and divides it by the actual cash you put in: the down payment plus purchase tax and closing costs, not the property's full price. On this property, ₪750,000 down plus roughly ₪120,000 in purchase tax at the investment-property rate (see our [purchase tax deep dive](/blog/purchase-tax-brackets-explained) for how that's calculated) comes to about ₪870,000 invested, for a cash on cash return of under 1%. That's the number that reflects what your actual leveraged investment is earning, and it's often the least flattering of the three, precisely because it's the most complete.

## Why all three numbers can tell a different story

A property can have a respectable gross yield, a decent net yield, and still barely cash flow, or even lose money monthly, once real financing terms are applied. That's not a contradiction, it's what leverage does: it can amplify your return relative to the cash you put in, but it can just as easily turn a modest yield into thin or negative monthly cash flow if the loan payment is a big enough share of the rent.

## Debt service coverage: a quick sanity check

One more number worth knowing: debt service coverage ratio, monthly rent divided by monthly mortgage payment. Above 1 means the rent alone covers the payment before any other costs; below 1 means it doesn't, and you'd be covering part of the mortgage from your own pocket every month even before insurance or maintenance. In the example above, ₪5,600 divided by ₪4,606 is about 1.22, rent alone covers the payment with some room to spare, though the recurring costs still eat into the cushion.

## Frequently asked questions

### What's the difference between gross yield and net yield?

Gross annual yield is simply annual rent divided by the property price, nothing else. Net annual yield subtracts your recurring ownership costs, insurance, maintenance, and management fees, from the rent before dividing by the price. Both deliberately exclude the mortgage payment.

### What is cash on cash return for an investment property?

It's your annual net cash flow (rent minus mortgage payment minus recurring costs) divided by the actual cash you invested, the down payment plus purchase tax and closing costs, not the property's full price. It reflects what your leveraged investment is actually earning.

### What does a debt service coverage ratio below 1 mean?

It means monthly rent alone doesn't cover the monthly mortgage payment. You'd be covering part of the mortgage from your own pocket every month, even before insurance or maintenance costs are added in.
