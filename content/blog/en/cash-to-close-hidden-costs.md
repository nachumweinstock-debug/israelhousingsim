Buyers who plan their savings purely around the down payment implied by their LTV ceiling are almost always underestimating how much cash they actually need on closing day. The gap is closing costs, and in Israel they're substantial enough to deserve their own line item in your planning, not an afterthought.

## Purchase tax (Mas Rechisha)

Mas rechisha is a tiered tax on the purchase price, and the brackets and rates depend heavily on your buyer category:

- **A single home** (your only property) gets the most favorable brackets, often with a 0% band on the lowest portion of the price before rates step up.
- **An additional home** (you already own one) faces a materially higher flat-ish rate structure from the first shekel, with no equivalent 0% band.
- **New immigrants** get their own separate bracket structure for a property purchased within the eligible window after aliyah. In practice a bank or advisor should check both schedules and use whichever comes out lower for your specific price, since which one actually wins depends on where your price falls (see the [deep dive on purchase tax brackets](/blog/purchase-tax-brackets-explained) for the worked math).

Because it's bracket-based like income tax, the effective rate on the full price is usually well below the top marginal bracket, but it can still run from a token amount into the tens of thousands of shekels depending on price and category. This is not optional and cannot be rolled into the mortgage: it's due directly to the tax authority, typically within 60 days of the transaction.

## Legal fees

You'll need your own lawyer for the transaction (this isn't optional in practice, even though it isn't strictly a legal requirement), typically running around 0.5-1.5% of the purchase price plus VAT, though it varies by firm and complexity. Your lawyer handles the title search, contract review, registration, and coordination with the bank's own legal requirements for releasing the mortgage funds. This is separate from, and in addition to, the bank's own legal or administrative fees for processing the loan itself.

## Agent commission

If you used a real estate agent (common, though not universal, in Israeli transactions), commission is typically around 2% of the price plus VAT, often paid by the buyer, the seller, or split, depending on how the deal was structured. Confirm this explicitly before you're deep into a transaction, since assuming the seller covers it when your agreement says otherwise is a common and expensive misunderstanding.

## Appraisal and registration fees

The bank will require an independent appraisal (shamaut) of the property before finalizing your loan. This is a real cost you pay directly, not something the bank absorbs, typically a flat fee rather than a percentage. There are also smaller registration and administrative fees tied to recording the transaction and the mortgage lien with the Land Registry (Tabu).

## Adding it up

As a rough planning heuristic, budget an additional 3-6% of the property price on top of your down payment to cover purchase tax, legal fees, agent commission, and the smaller appraisal/registration costs. The exact figure depends heavily on your buyer category (purchase tax brackets swing this the most) and whether an agent was involved. On a ₪2,000,000 property, that's commonly an additional ₪60,000-₪120,000 in cash beyond your down payment, due on closing, none of it financeable through the mortgage itself.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much cash do I need beyond my down payment?

Budget an additional 3-6% of the property price to cover purchase tax, legal fees, agent commission, and appraisal/registration costs. On a ₪2,000,000 property, that's commonly ₪60,000-₪120,000 beyond the down payment itself.

### Can closing costs be rolled into the mortgage?

No. Purchase tax, legal fees, agent commission, and appraisal costs cannot be financed into the loan. They're due directly, in cash, separate from your mortgage.

### Who pays the agent commission, the buyer or the seller?

It varies by deal, commonly paid by the buyer, the seller, or split, depending on how the transaction was structured. Confirm this explicitly before you're deep into a deal, since assuming the seller covers it when the agreement says otherwise is a common and expensive misunderstanding.
