Refinancing, mihzur mashkanta, means replacing your existing mortgage, or a track within it, with a new loan at a new rate. When rates have moved meaningfully since you took your loan, it's worth checking. But "rates dropped" isn't the same as "refinancing pays off," and the gap between those two is entirely the cost side of the equation.

## What refinancing actually costs

Two categories of cost show up whenever you refinance:

- **The early repayment penalty on the loan you're replacing**, if any of it sits in fixed tracks (kalatz or katz). We cover exactly how that penalty is calculated in our [early repayment guide](/blog/early-repayment-penalty-kalatz-katz), the short version is that it depends on how your old locked rate compares to current market rates, not a flat percentage.
- **New loan setup costs**, generally a new appraisal, since the bank needs a current valuation regardless of who's lending, plus typically a smaller arrangement or administrative fee from the new bank. Ask both your current bank and the prospective new one for their exact fee schedule rather than assuming a number, since these vary by lender and aren't standardized the way LTV ceilings are.

## The comparison that actually matters: breakeven time

The method is simple even though the exact figures are specific to you: divide your total refinancing cost by your monthly payment savings, and that tells you how many months it takes before the savings actually outweigh what you spent to get them.

Here's the mechanic worked through with illustrative numbers. Say you have ₪900,000 remaining on a track with 20 years left, currently at 6%. Refinancing that portion down to 5% at the same remaining term drops the payment from roughly ₪6,448 a month to roughly ₪5,940, a savings of about ₪508 a month. Now say, purely for illustration, that the early repayment penalty plus the new loan's fees together come to roughly ₪15,000. Dividing that cost by the monthly savings gives a breakeven of about 30 months, two and a half years.

If you're confident you'll hold the property, and the loan, for longer than that breakeven period, refinancing is likely worth it. If you might sell or refinance again before then, for instance because you're not sure how long you'll stay in the property, the penalty and fees may eat most or all of the benefit before you ever collect it.

## Don't just compare the headline rate

A new rate that's lower on paper doesn't always mean a lower total cost, for two reasons that are easy to miss:

- **Term reset.** If refinancing resets your term back up (for example, refinancing a loan with 15 years left into a fresh 25-year term), your monthly payment can drop even without genuine savings, simply because you're spreading the same or a similar balance over more months. That can look like a win on your monthly budget while actually costing more in total interest over the life of the loan.
- **CPI-linked balances.** If any of the balance you're refinancing is on a katz track, remember the *balance itself*, not just the rate, has been growing with inflation since you took the loan. Make sure the quote you're comparing against reflects the actual current balance, not the original loan amount.

## When refinancing usually doesn't make sense

If rates have moved only slightly, if you're already near the end of your term, or if most of your mix sits in prime (which already floats with the market and generally has no comparable early repayment penalty to escape), the breakeven math often doesn't clear a meaningful bar. Run the numbers before assuming a rate headline in the news means you should act.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I know if refinancing my Israeli mortgage is worth it?

Divide your total refinancing cost, the early repayment penalty plus new loan fees, by your monthly payment savings. That gives you a breakeven in months. If you'll hold the property and loan longer than that, refinancing is likely worth it.

### Does a lower rate always mean refinancing saves money?

Not always. Watch for two traps: a term reset that lowers your monthly payment without real savings by spreading the balance over more months, and CPI-linked balances, where the current balance being refinanced may already be larger than the original loan amount.

### When does refinancing usually not make sense?

When rates have moved only slightly, when you're already near the end of your term, or when most of your mix sits in prime, which already floats with the market and generally has no comparable early repayment penalty to escape.
