You don't need a mortgage advisor (yoetz mashkantaot) to get a mashkanta in Israel, plenty of buyers go straight to a bank and get a perfectly reasonable loan. But advisors exist for real reasons, and knowing what they actually do helps you decide whether paying for one makes sense for your situation.

## What an advisor actually does

An independent mortgage advisor works for you, not for any single bank, and typically handles:

- **Shopping your loan across multiple banks simultaneously**, rather than you separately visiting and negotiating with each one, which is genuinely time consuming and often gets you inconsistent offers that are hard to compare apples to apples.
- **Structuring your track mix**, translating your risk tolerance and plans into an actual prime, kalatz, katz split, similar to the framework in our [fixed vs. variable post](/blog/fixed-vs-variable-track-mix), but tailored with real quotes rather than general principles.
- **Negotiating rates**, since banks often have more room to move on their initial quote than they lead with, and an advisor who does this regularly across many clients typically has a better sense of what's actually negotiable than a first-time buyer walking in alone.
- **Handling paperwork and timing**, coordinating the various documents and deadlines across multiple bank applications so nothing falls through because of a missed requirement.

A bank's own in-house advisor, by contrast, only shows you that bank's own products, however good those happen to be. That's not dishonest, it's simply a structurally different incentive than an independent advisor shopping across the whole market.

## Roughly what it costs

Independent advisors are typically compensated either through a flat fee or a percentage of the loan amount, paid by you, the borrower, not the bank. The exact structure and rate vary by advisor and aren't standardized the way regulatory figures like LTV ceilings are, so confirm the specific fee arrangement directly with any advisor you're considering before engaging them, rather than assuming a number from a general estimate. Ask upfront whether the fee is contingent on closing a loan, flat regardless of outcome, or structured some other way, since this varies by advisor.

## When it's worth it

An advisor tends to earn their fee back when:

- **Your situation is more complex than a standard case**, foreign income, self-employment, an oleh chadash timing question, or a replacing-home bridge scenario are all cases where the specific expertise can matter more than it would for a straightforward salaried first-time buyer.
- **You genuinely don't have the time to shop multiple banks yourself**, and would otherwise just accept the first reasonable offer rather than compare.
- **The loan is large enough that a modest rate improvement is worth meaningfully more, in absolute shekels, than the advisor's fee.** A small rate improvement on a large loan can be worth far more over the term than the same improvement on a small one.

## When handling it yourself is probably fine

If your situation is straightforward (stable salaried income, standard first-home purchase, no unusual timing constraints) and you have the time and patience to get quotes from a few banks yourself, the advisor's main value, comparison shopping, is something you can reasonably do on your own. Walking in already knowing your numbers, your target mix, and roughly what a competitive rate looks like closes most of the gap between going in cold and having someone negotiate for you.
