Two insurance policies stand between you and the bank actually releasing your loan funds, and neither is optional. Both protect the bank's collateral, in different ways, and both are worth understanding before closing day rather than being surprised by them.

## Mortgage life insurance (bituach chayim)

This covers the outstanding loan balance if a borrower dies during the term. If you have a co-applicant, both of you are typically required to carry coverage, usually proportional to each person's share of the loan or income. The payout goes toward paying down or clearing the mortgage balance, protecting your family from being left with the debt, and protecting the bank's collateral position at the same time.

The cost depends heavily on individual factors: age, health, smoking status, and the loan amount all move the premium, the same way any life insurance premium works. It's typically structured as a recurring cost (monthly or annual) tied to the declining loan balance over time, rather than a flat one-time fee. Because it's individually underwritten, there's no single "typical" percentage worth quoting here, confirm your actual premium with an insurance provider or your bank's in-house offering once you have real health and loan details to underwrite against, rather than budgeting off a generic estimate.

## Structure insurance (bituach mivne)

This covers the physical building itself against damage, fire and structural risks being the core coverage, protecting the property that serves as the bank's collateral for the loan. This is different from contents insurance (covering your belongings) and different from the mortgage life policy above, it exists purely to make sure the asset backing the loan is protected.

Cost here is driven mainly by the property's rebuild value (not its market price, which includes land and location value that wouldn't need "rebuilding" after damage), its construction type and age, and its location. Again, this varies enough by property that a specific percentage isn't a reliable planning number, get a quote against your actual property once you have an address and rebuild estimate.

## Why the bank requires both, specifically

Both policies exist to protect the same thing from two different angles: the bank's collateral. Structure insurance protects the physical asset from being damaged or destroyed with nothing to show for the loan. Life insurance protects the loan from becoming unpayable if a borrower dies. A bank won't release funds without proof both are in place, and will typically require the bank itself (or its designated interest) be listed as a beneficiary or loss payee on both policies for the life of the loan.

## Can you use your own provider, or does it have to be the bank's?

In most cases you can shop these policies independently rather than accepting whatever the bank's in-house or partner insurer quotes, as long as the policy meets the bank's coverage requirements and correctly names the bank's interest. It's worth getting an independent quote to compare against the bank's offer specifically because banks don't always have a strong incentive to shop for you the cheapest option, confirm this is genuinely optional at your specific bank before assuming it, since practices can vary.

## Where this fits in your budget

Unlike purchase tax or agent commission, these aren't one-time closing costs, they're ongoing costs that ride alongside your mortgage payment for the life of the loan (life insurance premiums typically decline over time as the balance you're insuring shrinks). Factor them into your monthly affordability picture, not just your cash-to-close figure, alongside the [other closing costs](/blog/cash-to-close-hidden-costs) that hit before the mortgage even starts.
