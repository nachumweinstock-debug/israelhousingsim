If you've spoken to an Israeli bank about a mashkanta, you've heard some combination of "prime," "kalatz," and "katz" thrown around quickly, as if you already knew what they meant. Here's the plain version.

## Why there are tracks at all

Unlike a typical US-style single fixed-rate mortgage, an Israeli mashkanta is built by combining several loan "tracks," each with a different rate structure, and the Bank of Israel requires banks to offer you a mix across track types rather than letting you put 100% into one. The regulatory logic is that no single rate structure is safe at 100% exposure: a fully variable loan is dangerously exposed to rate hikes, and a fully fixed loan can lock you out of ever benefiting from falling rates. A required mix spreads that risk.

## Prime (Ribit Prime)

Prime-linked tracks float with the Bank of Israel's prime lending rate, which itself moves with the Bank of Israel's base interest rate. When the central bank raises rates to fight inflation, your prime-track payment rises within the same billing cycle or shortly after, with no lag like there is with the fixed tracks. The upside is that prime tracks usually start with the lowest headline rate of the three track types, and if rates fall, you benefit immediately too. Regulation caps how much of your total mix can sit in a track that reprices at any time (this is largely what prime tracks are), which is why you'll never see a 100% prime offer from a compliant bank.

## Kalatz (Ribit Kvuah Lo Tzmuda): Fixed, Not Linked to the CPI

Kalatz is a fixed-rate track that is not linked to inflation (CPI). Your rate and your shekel payment are locked for the life of that track's term. This is the closest thing to the fixed-rate mortgage many olim are used to from other countries: totally predictable, no exposure to either interest rate moves or inflation. The tradeoff is that the starting rate on a kalatz track is usually higher than prime, since the bank is pricing in the certainty it's giving up.

## Katz (Ribit Kvuah Tzmuda): Fixed, But Linked to the CPI

Katz is also a fixed rate, but the *principal balance itself* is indexed to the Consumer Price Index. Every time the CPI rises, your outstanding balance is adjusted upward before that fixed rate is applied, meaning your shekel payment can still grow over time even though the interest rate never changes, purely because inflation is expanding the loan balance it's calculated against. This surprises a lot of first-time buyers who hear "fixed rate" and assume a flat payment for the life of the loan. It doesn't work that way for katz tracks. Over a 20-25 year term in a normal inflation environment, the CPI-linked growth in the balance is often the single biggest driver of how much more you pay than the headline rate would suggest.

## Reading a track quote

For any track (except unlinked fixed, which never resets), you'll typically see two numbers worth paying attention to: the payment today, and the projected payment after a reset or after a stress-tested rate move. A rate that looks attractive today can look very different once you account for a plausible 1-2 point rise in Prime, or a decade of CPI compounding on a katz balance. Comparing "payment today" across tracks in isolation, without looking at those projected figures, is the most common mistake people make when choosing a mix.

## How the pieces usually fit together

There's no universally "correct" mix. It depends on your risk tolerance, how long you plan to hold the property, and your view on where rates are headed. As a rough pattern: buyers who want maximum stability lean more heavily into kalatz, buyers comfortable with some rate exposure in exchange for a lower starting payment lean into prime, and katz often ends up as the balancing third leg, since it isn't directly rate-sensitive the way prime is, but does carry its own inflation exposure.
