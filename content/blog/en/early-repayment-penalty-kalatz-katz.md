If you've ever asked a bank what it would cost to pay off your mashkanta early, and gotten back a number bigger than you expected, you've run into kenas pira'on mukdam, the early repayment penalty. It's real, it's regulated, and it doesn't apply the way most people assume.

## The core idea: it's about the rate gap, not a flat fee

The penalty exists because a fixed-rate loan is, from the bank's side, a bet on future interest rates. When you lock in a rate on a kalatz or katz track (see our [explainer on the three tracks](/blog/prime-kalatz-katz-tracks-explained) if those terms are new), the bank is committing to that rate for the term, regardless of what happens to rates afterward. If you repay early while your locked rate is *higher* than the rate the bank could currently lend that same money out at, the bank is losing the future interest income it was counting on, and the penalty is designed to compensate for roughly that difference.

The practical result: whether you owe a meaningful penalty at all depends mostly on which direction rates have moved since you took the loan, not on some fixed percentage of your balance.

- If your locked rate is **higher** than current comparable market rates, expect a real penalty. The bigger the gap and the longer you have left on the term, the larger it tends to be.
- If your locked rate is **lower** than current comparable market rates, the penalty is often minimal or effectively zero, since the bank isn't losing anything by getting its money back early when it could relend it at a better rate anyway.

## Which tracks this actually applies to

This penalty is specific to fixed-rate tracks, kalatz and katz. A prime-linked track reprices with the market on its own, so there's generally no comparable early repayment penalty on the prime portion of your mix, since the bank was never locked into an above-market rate on that money in the first place.

## Getting your real number

Because the calculation depends on a bank-published reference rate at the moment you actually repay, not a number you can look up in advance, the only way to know your real penalty is to request an official early repayment quote (often called a yitrat siluk in Hebrew) from your bank. Banks are required to provide this on request, and it will show the exact payoff amount including any penalty, as of that specific date. There's often a smaller fixed administrative fee added on top as well. Treat any number you calculate yourself as a rough estimate for planning purposes only, and get the official quote before you commit to anything.

## When paying it off early is still worth it

A penalty being real doesn't automatically mean early repayment is a bad move. The comparison that actually matters is the penalty (plus any fees) against what you gain by repaying, which usually falls into one of a few categories:

- **Refinancing into a materially lower rate.** If the new rate saves you enough per month, the savings can outrun the penalty within a few years. This is the same math covered in our [refinancing guide](/blog/refinancing-israeli-mortgage), and the penalty is exactly the "cost" side of that comparison.
- **Selling the property.** If you're selling anyway, the penalty is simply a cost of the sale, not something you can avoid by waiting, and it's usually smaller relative to your total proceeds than it feels in isolation.
- **A windfall you'd otherwise leave sitting in a low-yield account.** If the alternative use of that cash earns less than your locked mortgage rate, paying down the loan even with a penalty can still come out ahead over time, though this is a personal-finance judgment call, not a universal rule.
