Our [cash-to-close post](/blog/cash-to-close-hidden-costs) mentioned purchase tax as one line among several. It deserves its own explanation, because almost everyone gets the same thing wrong about it: they assume the whole price gets taxed at whichever bracket it reaches. It doesn't. Mas rechisha works the same way income tax does, only the portion of the price inside each bracket gets that bracket's rate.

## The three schedules

Israel runs three separate bracket schedules, and which one applies depends on your buyer category:

- **Single home** (this will be your only property): the most favorable schedule, with a 0% band on the lowest portion of the price, then rising marginal rates above that.
- **Additional home** (you already own at least one property): a materially higher flat-ish rate from the first shekel, with no 0% band at all.
- **New immigrant**: its own separate flat-ish schedule, available for a property bought within the eligible window around your aliyah date.

These exact thresholds are set by law and periodically revised (the current set is scheduled for review in early 2028), so treat them as current as of when this was written, not a permanent fact. Confirm the live thresholds with a bank or the Israel Tax Authority before relying on them for a real purchase. The single-home schedule referenced below currently runs 0% up to about ₪1,978,745, then 3.5% up to about ₪2,347,040, then 5% up to about ₪6,055,070, then higher bands above that.

## The marginal math, worked through

Say you're buying your only home for ₪3,000,000. Working through the single-home bands:

1. The first ₪1,978,745 of the price: taxed at 0%. Tax owed: ₪0.
2. The next band, from there up to ₪2,347,040: taxed at 3.5%. That's ₪368,295 in this band, so roughly ₪12,890.
3. The remaining amount from there up to your ₪3,000,000 price: taxed at 5%. That's ₪652,960 in this band, so roughly ₪32,648.

Add the bands together and the total comes to roughly ₪45,538, well under 2% of the full price, even though the price reached a bracket whose own rate is higher than that. That's the entire point of marginal brackets: only the slice of the price inside each band pays that band's rate, the same way a raise that pushes you into a higher income tax bracket doesn't tax your whole salary at the new rate, only the portion above the threshold.

Compare that to an additional home at the same ₪3,000,000 price. That schedule currently runs a flat 8% from the first shekel up to about ₪6,055,070, with no 0% band at all, so the same ₪3,000,000 purchase owes roughly ₪240,000, over five times the single-home result, for the identical purchase price. Buyer category alone can be the difference between a tax bill in the tens of thousands and one several times that size.

## Where the new immigrant schedule actually helps, and where it doesn't

This is the part that surprises people. The new immigrant schedule is usually described as "more favorable," and at very high prices, it can be, since it currently runs a flat 5% above roughly ₪1,988,090 with no further step-ups, while the single-home schedule keeps climbing to 8% and then 10% at higher prices. But at the price ranges most first-time buyers are actually shopping in, the comparison flips. On that same ₪3,000,000 example, the new immigrant schedule (0.5% up to ₪1,988,090, 5% above) comes to roughly ₪60,536, which is actually *higher* than the ₪45,538 a regular resident pays under the single-home schedule at that same price. A properly built calculation should check both schedules for your specific price and use whichever is lower, since which one wins depends entirely on where your price falls, not on a fixed rule that one schedule always beats the other. Don't assume the immigrant schedule is automatically the cheaper path without actually running both numbers. ## Why this matters for your cash-to-close budget

Purchase tax is due directly to the tax authority, typically within a couple of months of the transaction, and it cannot be rolled into the mortgage. Underestimating it because you assumed a flat rate on the full price, rather than working through the actual bands, is one of the more common ways buyers get caught short on closing day.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is purchase tax calculated on the full price at the top bracket rate?

No. Purchase tax is marginal, like income tax. Only the portion of the price that falls inside each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate, so the effective rate on the full price is usually well below the top marginal bracket you reach.

### Do new immigrants always pay less purchase tax than regular residents?

No. At typical first-home prices, the standard single-home schedule often comes out equal to or better than the new immigrant schedule. The immigrant schedule only pulls ahead at very high prices. Always check both schedules for your specific price.

### When is purchase tax due, and can it be included in the mortgage?

It's due directly to the tax authority, typically within about two months of the transaction, and it cannot be rolled into the mortgage. It's a separate cash obligation from your loan.
