If you've started pricing out a mashkanta, you've probably already run into the term LTV — loan-to-value. It's the single biggest number in Israeli mortgage planning, because it decides the minimum cash you need before a bank will even talk to you.

## What LTV actually means

LTV is simply the loan amount divided by the property's price (or appraised value, if lower). A ₪1,400,000 loan on a ₪2,000,000 property is 70% LTV. The remaining 30%, ₪600,000, is your equity — your down payment plus any other cash you're bringing to closing.

Israeli banks don't set this number loan by loan. The Bank of Israel sets hard ceilings that every bank in the country has to follow, and the ceiling depends entirely on your buyer category.

## The three ceilings

- **First home in Israel:** up to 75% LTV. You need at least 25% equity.
- **Replacing your home** (selling one property to buy another, and it'll be your only home once the sale closes): up to 70% LTV. You need at least 30% equity.
- **Investment property or an additional home:** up to 50% LTV. You need at least 50% equity.

These are ceilings, not entitlements — a bank can approve you for less than the maximum based on your income, credit, and the property itself, but it legally cannot lend you more than the ceiling for your category, no matter how strong your file is.

## Why the ceiling changes by category

The logic is risk, not fairness. A first-time buyer financing their only home is the profile regulators most want to protect and enable — hence the highest ceiling. An investor buying a second or third property is taking on discretionary risk, and a downturn in that property's value doesn't threaten their housing, so the ceiling is tighter to keep leverage in check across the system.

If you're an oleh chadash (new immigrant), you may qualify for additional financing benefits on top of the first-home ceiling — that's specific enough to deserve its own explanation, which we cover in [our oleh chadash mortgage guide](/blog/oleh-chadash-mortgage-benefits).

## Why appraised value matters, not just the price you agreed on

LTV is calculated against whichever is lower: the purchase price or the bank's own appraisal. If you agree to pay ₪2,000,000 for a property but the bank's appraiser values it at ₪1,900,000, your maximum loan is 75% of ₪1,900,000, not ₪2,000,000. That gap comes out of your pocket as extra equity. It's one of the more common surprises in the process, and it's a good reason to budget a buffer above the regulatory minimum rather than planning to the exact edge.

## Working backward from LTV to your down payment

Once you know your category's ceiling, the math is simple: your minimum down payment is the property price times (1 minus the LTV ceiling). On a ₪2,000,000 first home at the 75% ceiling, that's ₪500,000 minimum. Add closing costs on top — purchase tax, legal fees, and agent commission typically run another 3-5% of the price beyond the down payment itself, and those don't count toward your LTV, they're pure additional cash.
